The Potter’s Lesson: Mastery in Detached Action

बुद्धियुक्तो जहातीह उभे सुकृतदुष्कृते |
तस्माद्योगाय युज्यस्व योगः कर्मसु कौशलम् ||

Verse Source:
Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 2, Verse 50

English Translation:
“One endowed with wisdom (or discerning intellect) casts aside both good and bad deeds in this world. Therefore, engage in the practice of yoga, for yoga is skill in action.”

Transliteration:
“Buddhyāyukto jahātīha ubhe sukṛta-duṣhkṛite;
Tasmād yogāya yujyasva yogah karmasu kauśhalam.”

Grammatical Analysis:

  • “Buddhyāyukto” is an adjective describing someone endowed with intelligence.
  • “jahātīha” is a verb indicating the action of giving up in this world.
  • “ubhe sukṛta-duṣhkṛite” denotes the dual nature of deeds, both good and bad, and is in the locative case pointing to the location or context in which the action of giving up is taking place.
  • “yujyasva” is in the imperative mood, suggesting a command or instruction to engage in yoga.

Additional grammatical features:

  • Mood: Imperative in “yujyasva”.
  • Compound type: Dvandva compound in “sukṛta-duṣhkṛite”.

Phonetic Features:

  • Sandhi rules apply in “Buddhyā-yukto” where “ā” and “y” combine.
  • Visarga sandhi in “Tasmād yogāya” where the “d” of “Tasmād” modifies the following word.

Synonyms and Alternate Meanings:

  • “Yoga” can also mean union or connection.
  • “Kauśhalam” can refer to expertise or proficiency.

Cultural or Philosophical Significance:

This verse highlights the importance of equanimity and detachment. Even while performing actions, one should remain unattached to the results, considering them neither good nor bad. This is the essence of karma yoga, the yoga of action. Acting with skill and intelligence without attachment to outcomes ensures liberation.

The Potter’s Lesson:


In a tranquil village nestled between mountains, lived a wise potter named Vimal. Vimal was renowned not just for the exquisite pots he crafted but also for the serene manner in which he worked. Children would often gather around his workshop, not to watch the pots take shape, but to observe the calm demeanor of the potter.

One day, a curious child asked Vimal, “Why do you always seem so peaceful, even if the pot breaks or doesn’t come out as expected?” Vimal smiled and replied, “I do my work with complete focus and dedication, giving my best to every pot I craft. But once it’s done, I don’t dwell on how it turns out. Like in the teachings of the Bhagavad Gita, I believe that yoga is skill in action. It’s not about the result but the dedication and skill you put into the action.”

His words resonated with the villagers, reminding them of the essence of Karma Yoga from the Bhagavad Gita - to act with skill and dedication, without attachment to the outcomes.

"In the stillness of meditation, even our most cherished creations crumble, teaching us the beauty of detachment."
